What is Skin Cancer?

Skin cancer is a serious skin cell disease caused by overexposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation from the sun. The structure of our skin cells changes every time our skin is exposed to UV radiation. Excessive exposure to UV radiation can cause irreversible damage to skin cell DNA, and one damaged cell can cause skin cancer to increase.

Types of Skin Cancer

Basal Cell Carcinoma
Squamous Cell Carcinoma
Melanoma

Basal Cell Carcinoma

BCC accounts for about 70% of non-melanoma skin cancers. It begins in the lower layer of the epidermis (outer layer of the skin). It can appear anywhere on the body but mostly develops on parts of the body that receive high or intermittent sun exposure (head, neck, shoulders, back).

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

SCC accounts for about 30% of non-melanoma skin cancers. It begins in the upper layer of the epidermis and usually appears where the skin has had most exposure to the sun (head, face, neck, hands, forearms and lower legs). SCC generally grows quickly over weeks or months.

Melanoma

Often melanoma has no symptoms, however, the first sign is generally a change in an existing mole or the appearance of a new spot. Rare melanomas can occur inside the eye or in parts of the skin or body that have never been exposed to the sun.

1,960

In 2016, 1,960 people died from skin cancer in Australia, 1281 from melanoma and 679 from non-melanoma skin cancers.

2 in 3

Approximately, two in three Australians will be diagnosed with skin cancer by the time they are 70.

13,694

Melanoma is the third most common cancer in Australians. In 2015, 13,694 Australians were diagnosed with melanoma.

Symptoms

Get familiar with your skin's look, so you'll pick up any changes that might indicate skin cancer. Look for:

Freckles, moles, spots that change in color, size or shape.

Crusty, non-healing lesion.

Red, pale or pearly small lumps.

Causes

Exposure to UV radiation is the main reason of skin cancer.

Sunburn

Many people get sunburnt when they participate in beach or pool water sports and activities, as well as having a barbecue or gardening.

Tanning

A tan is not a sign of good health or well-being, despite the reference made by many Australians to a "healthy tan."

Solarium

Commercial solariums have been banned in all Australian states as of January 2016. Commercial solariums were also banned by ACT.

Prevention

To protect the skin from UV lights; experts recommend the following sun protection measures:

Apply broad spectrum, water resistant sunscreen

Slip on some sun-protective clothing

Slide on some sunglasses, slap on a hat

Seek shade, don't get sunburned

Diagnosis

Your GP will examine you in most cases, paying attention to any spots that might seem suspicious.

It is very important to go through a do-it-yourself skin examination and consult a doctor in case of any change. Almost always, skin cancers are eliminated. Some of the surrounding tissue may also be extracted in more advanced skin cancers to ensure that all cancer cells have been removed.
